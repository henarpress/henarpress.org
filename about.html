About Us

Our Story

Like most Kurds in the diaspora, I come from a family that's moved around a lot. My parents fled Iraqi-Kurdistan during Saddam's Anfal campaign and landed in Norway as asylum seekers. I spent my childhood moving from one country to another (often across continents), never quite settling anywhere.

As I grew older and began reading more deeply, I searched for novels that reflected my identity, but they were hard to find. Impossible, actually. I had to wait until 2016 to read the first Kurdish novel available in English, Bakhtiyar Ali's I Stared at the Night of the City, masterfully translated by Kareem Abdulrahman.

Reading about Sulaymaniyah was a profoundly moving experience. Great fiction has the ability to change your life, and this book did exactly that.

I immersed myself in all the Kurdish literature I could find. Jalal Barzanji's The Man in Blue Pyjamas, Choman Hardi's Life for Us, Ava Homa's Daughters of Smoke and Fire, among many more incredible works.

But after finishing those books, I was left with nothing. I'd read everything I could find in a matter of months. It was frustrating knowing that there were more great Kurdish writers out there. So, my wife and I decided to start Henar Press.

We're a nonprofit, independent publisher based in Columbus, Ohio, dedicated to translating and publishing the best of Kurdish literature. We're excited to share with English readers what we believe is one of the literary world's best kept secrets.

What does Henar mean?

Henar is the Kurdish word for pomegranate. It's the national symbol of Kurdistan and has been a recurring motif in Kurdish literature since Mem û Zîn. We were personally drawn to the pomegranate tree's ability to flourish in the harshest conditions.

Who are the Kurds?

The Kurds are an indigenous people spanning Turkey, Iraq, Iran, and Syria. For centuries, we've faced persecution and colonization, from the Ottoman Empire's forced assimilation to the Ba'athist ethnic cleansing in Iraq. Despite efforts to erase our identity, we've maintained a strong determination to preserve our languages, traditions, and culture.

What do you mean by Against Forgetting, We Write?

We write to remember, to resist, and to reclaim our stories. Our words honor the memories of Halabja, the Anfal campaign, and the countless Kurdish writers imprisoned and killed for their work.

Do all Kurds speak the same language?

While Kurish is often referred to as a single language with multiple dialects, it's more accurately a family of distinct languages. For example, a Sorani speaker wouldn't understand Kurmanji, and vice versa.
